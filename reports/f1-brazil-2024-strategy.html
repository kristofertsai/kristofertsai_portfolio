F1 STRATEGY ANALYSIS · CASE 02 · BRAZIL 2024

Brazil 2024: Recovery, Strategy and Conversion

Max Verstappen won the 2024 São Paulo Grand Prix after starting P17 in difficult wet conditions. This study separates the contribution of his opening recovery, the Lap 28 pit decisions, the subsequent red flag and his pace after the final restart.

Research question

How much of Max Verstappen’s P17-to-P1 victory came from on-track recovery, strategic positioning and the red flag?

Independent analysis using publicly available data. Not affiliated with Formula 1 or any Formula 1 team.

  • Wet-weather strategy
  • Position reconstruction
  • Red-flag scenario
  • Post-restart pace

Executive summary

Race reconstruction

The Recovery Was Already Underway

Verstappen gained six positions on the opening lap, moving from P17 to P11. He then gained another six positions through the wet-weather opening phase, reaching P5 before the Virtual Safety Car on Lap 28. Twelve of his sixteen total positions had therefore been gained before the central strategic sequence.

Key distinction

The red flag protected track position; it did not create the three-place gain

The pit decisions moved Verstappen from P5 to P2. The red flag then preserved that track position by allowing the stay-out group to change tyres without a conventional pit-lane loss.

Critical limitation

This is not a certain classification without the red flag

This is a same-window Lap 28 stop scenario. Without the red flag, the stay-out drivers may instead have stopped later under the Safety Car, which could have reduced their position loss. The model therefore shows the potential value of the free tyre change, not a definitive alternate race result.

Verdict

Verstappen’s victory came from three distinct contributions

  1. 01

    On-track recovery

    He moved from P17 to P5 before the VSC, gaining twelve positions without relying on the decisive strategic sequence.

  2. 02

    Strategic positioning

    The Lap 28 stops ahead moved Verstappen from P5 to P2. The red flag then let the stay-out group replace its tyres without surrendering that position through a conventional stop.

  3. 03

    Race conversion

    After the second restart, Verstappen was approximately 0.70 to 0.97 seconds per lap faster than the comparison drivers and turned the strategic opportunity into a decisive victory.

The red flag was materially beneficial, but it neither created Verstappen’s opening recovery nor directly moved him from P5 to P2. Strategy placed him in a race-winning position; his final-stint pace completed the win.

Transparency

Methodology and limitations

Data source

Public OpenF1 data for the 2024 São Paulo Grand Prix, including:

  • Lap timing and driver positions
  • Tyre stints and pit stops
  • Race-control messages
  • Session results

Methods

  • Lap-by-lap position reconstruction
  • Strategic checkpoint comparison
  • Position-gain decomposition
  • Same-window pit-loss counterfactual
  • Shared-lap post-restart pace comparison

Interpretive limits

  • The counterfactual does not simulate every Safety Car, tyre, traffic or restart outcome.
  • The modelled P7 assumes a Lap 28 stop comparable to Norris and Russell.
  • A later stop under the Safety Car may have cost fewer positions.
  • The cumulative pace chart resets drivers to zero and excludes actual starting gaps.
  • Public timing data cannot fully represent team information, driver confidence, visibility or real-time forecasts.
