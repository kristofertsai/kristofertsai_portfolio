F1 STRATEGY ANALYSIS · CASE 03 · FRANCE 2021

France 2021: When Did Verstappen’s Second Stop Become the Winning Strategy?

Red Bull surrendered the lead by stopping Max Verstappen for a second time on Lap 32. By the end of Lap 33, he was more than 18 seconds behind Lewis Hamilton, but had a substantial tyre-compound and tyre-age offset for the final 20 laps. This study reconstructs the required catch rate and tests the principal alternatives.

Research question

Did the advantage provided by the fresher medium tyres justify surrendering the lead, and was the second stop genuinely necessary to win?

Independent analysis using publicly available data. Not affiliated with Formula 1 or any Formula 1 team.

  • Two-stop strategy
  • Tyre degradation
  • Catch-rate analysis
  • Counterfactual analysis

Strategic context

The first stop won the lead; the second gave it back

Verstappen, Hamilton, Sergio Pérez and Valtteri Bottas began on used medium tyres. Bottas stopped on Lap 17, Verstappen stopped on Lap 18 and undercut Hamilton, and Hamilton responded on Lap 19. Verstappen led on hard tyres before Red Bull stopped him again on Lap 32 for another set of used medium tyres. Hamilton kept his hard tyres to the finish; Verstappen regained the lead on Lap 52 and won.

The second-stop break-even

Could Verstappen repay the second stop?

The measured pit cycle turned a 2.241-second lead at the end of Lap 31 into an 18.160-second deficit at the end of Lap 33. With 20 laps remaining, Verstappen needed an average gap-closure rate of approximately 0.91 seconds per lap to draw level by the finish.

Interpretation boundary

Drawing level is not the same as guaranteeing a pass

The 0.91 s/lap threshold is an arithmetic gap-closure rate. Traffic, overtaking difficulty and changing pace can still prevent a theoretical recovery from becoming a race-winning overtake.

Tyre-offset pace

How the tyre offset powered the chase

Across the same-lap comparison window from Laps 34–51, Verstappen averaged approximately 0.97 seconds per lap faster than Hamilton. The largest share of that measured advantage arrived in the first four laps; the advantage narrowed later but remained positive.

Pace attribution

The measured difference is not a tyre-only effect

The same-lap comparison reduces shared fuel-burn and track-evolution effects, but it cannot isolate the effects of car pace, driver performance, traffic, racecraft or changing race conditions.

Supporting counterfactual

Could Mercedes have covered the stop?

Applying Verstappen’s observed 20.401-second relative pit-cycle swing symmetrically to Hamilton suggests that an immediate Lap 33 response would have placed Hamilton approximately 2.241 seconds behind Verstappen. Over the remaining 20 laps, Hamilton would then have needed to close roughly 0.112 seconds per lap to draw level by the finish.

What the model can say

Whether a response would have worked remains uncertain—not clearly impossible

The observed opening-stint median was close to, but below, the nominal threshold. This simplified sensitivity does not establish what tyre sets Mercedes had available and does not account for car-specific pit-cycle costs, out-lap performance, traffic, overtaking difficulty or the pace either car would have produced.

One-stop sensitivity

Did Verstappen need the second stop?

At the end of Lap 31, Verstappen led by 2.241 seconds with 22 laps remaining. An average relative pace loss of less than approximately 0.102 seconds per lap would therefore have left him ahead at the finish under a constant-pace projection.

Evidence boundary

A plausible alternative is not a certain result

The second stop was not proven to be necessary. The pre-stop observations make staying out a plausible winning scenario under several constant-pace assumptions, but they cannot reveal how his hard tyres would have degraded or how tyre temperatures, traffic and defensive racing might have affected the outcome.

Final assessment

Successful, but not proven to be the only winning route

Red Bull’s second stop was aggressive and ultimately successful. It created a compound-and-age offset. After the stop, Verstappen erased an 18.2-second deficit and regained the lead on Lap 52. The public evidence does not, however, establish that it was the only viable winning strategy. His pre-stop hard-tyre pace suggests the one-stop may also have remained competitive in several sensitivity cases, but the unrun degradation, tyre temperatures, traffic and defensive racing cannot be reconstructed reliably.

Evidence supports

What the public data establishes

  • The second stop created a large and measurable compound-and-age offset.
  • Verstappen exceeded the required gap-closure rate.
  • The early chase accounted for the largest share of the observed gap reduction.
  • Verstappen completed the recovery and won after the second stop.

Evidence cannot prove

What remains counterfactual

  • That staying out would certainly have lost the race.
  • That a hypothetical Hamilton second stop would certainly have failed.
  • That tyre age alone caused the observed pace difference.
  • The exact unrun degradation of Verstappen’s hard tyres.

Transparency

Methodology and limitations

Data and gap reconstruction

  • Public FastF1 timing data
  • End-of-lap timing gaps
  • FastF1 laps flagged as accurate and run under green-flag conditions

Pace and scenario treatment

  • Lap 1, pit-in laps, pit-out laps, deleted laps and extreme timing anomalies excluded from pace summaries
  • Same-lap comparisons to reduce shared fuel-burn and track-evolution effects
  • Sensitivity analysis rather than deterministic prediction

Interpretive limits

  • Verstappen’s unrun hard-tyre performance cannot be observed directly.
  • Public data does not fully reveal tyre-set availability.
  • Traffic and overtaking are not fully modelled.
  • Car pace, driver execution and tyre condition are intertwined.
  • Counterfactual pit-cycle costs depend on simplifying assumptions.
